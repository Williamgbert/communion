The Sacrament of CommunionAlso known as the Eucharist, from the Greek word "eucharistia" meaning thanksgiving stands as the central act of Christian worship, instituted by Jesus Christ himself during the Last Supper.

Communion represents three profound realities:

  • Christ's Real Presence: The spiritual presence of Jesus in the bread and wine
  • Spiritual Nourishment: Food for the soul and strength for the Christian journey
  • Covenant Remembrance: A memorial of Christ's sacrifice and God's faithfulness
"And he took bread, gave thanks and broke it, and gave it to them, saying, 'This is my body given for you; do this in remembrance of me.'"
Luke 22:19

Throughout history, this sacred meal has united Christians across cultures, denominations, and centuries, serving as both a deeply personal encounter with Christ and a communal celebration of faith.

Cultural Context

The Last Supper took place during PassoverThe Jewish festival commemorating God's deliverance of Israel from Egypt. Jesus transformed this meal of remembrance into a new covenant celebration., connecting Christ's sacrifice to the ancient story of God's deliverance. The bread and wine were common elements of Jewish meals, making this sacrament accessible and meaningful to early Christians.

Early Liturgical Customs

  • Frequency: Weekly celebration on "the Lord's Day" (Sunday)
  • Preparation: Fasting and prayer before participation
  • Community: Shared meal emphasizing unity and equality
  • Elements: Bread and wine, often brought by the congregation

‚ú® Theological Themes

+

Four Central Themes

Real Presence

Christ is truly present in the Communion elements, not merely symbolically but spiritually and sacramentally. This presence transforms the ordinary bread and wine into sacred means of grace.

Sacrifice of Christ

Communion commemorates and makes present Christ's once-for-all sacrifice on the cross. It's not a re-sacrifice but a participation in the eternal offering of Christ.

Covenant Remembrance

The sacrament serves as a memorial (Greek: anamnesis) - not just remembering the past, but making the saving work of Christ present and active today.

Community Participation

Communion creates and expresses the unity of the church. As Paul writes, "Because there is one loaf, we, who are many, are one body" (1 Cor 10:17).

Deeper Insights from Early Christian Leaders

"He took from among creation that which is bread, and gave thanks, saying, 'This is my body.' The cup likewise, which is from among the creation to which we belong, he confessed to be his blood."
- Irenaeus, Against Heresies (180 AD)

Context: Irenaeus emphasizes that Christ uses ordinary created elements (bread and wine) to convey extraordinary spiritual realities, affirming both the goodness of creation and the reality of the incarnation.

"Let that be deemed a proper Eucharist, which is administered either by the bishop, or by one to whom he has entrusted it. Wherever the bishop shall appear, there let the multitude also be; even as, wherever Jesus Christ is, there is the Catholic Church."
- Ignatius of Antioch, Letter to the Smyrnaeans (110 AD)

Context: Ignatius connects proper celebration of the Eucharist with church unity and apostolic authority, emphasizing that Communion is not a private act but a communal celebration under proper leadership.

"If you are the body and members of Christ, then it is your sacrament that is placed on the table of the Lord; it is your sacrament that you receive. To that which you are you respond 'Amen' ('Yes, it is true!') and by responding to it you assent to it."
- Augustine, Sermon 272 (400 AD)

Context: Augustine beautifully explains how Christians both receive Christ in Communion and become what they receive - the body of Christ in the world.

"And on the day called Sunday, all who live in cities or in the country gather together to one place, and the memoirs of the apostles or the writings of the prophets are read... Then we all rise together and pray, and bread is brought, and wine and water, and the president in like manner offers prayers and thanksgivings."
- Justin Martyr, First Apology (150 AD)

Context: This provides one of our earliest detailed descriptions of Christian worship, showing how Scripture reading, prayer, and Communion formed the core of early Christian gatherings.

From the Didache (Teaching of the Twelve Apostles, ~100 AD)

"Now concerning the Eucharist, give thanks this way. First, concerning the cup: 'We thank thee, our Father, for the holy vine of David Thy servant, which You madest known to us through Jesus Thy Servant; to Thee be the glory forever.' And concerning the broken bread: 'We thank Thee, our Father, for the life and knowledge which You madest known to us through Jesus Thy Servant.'"
- Didache 9:1-3

Context: The Didache preserves some of the earliest Eucharistic prayers, emphasizing thanksgiving and the connection between the physical elements and spiritual realities of life and knowledge in Christ.
